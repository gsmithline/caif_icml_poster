\begin{abstract}
We conduct an empirical game-theoretic analysis of how large-language models negotiate the division of a set of subjectively valued items.
% with discounting over negotiation rounds and outside fallback options. 
The LLM agents represent \textit{meta-strategies}, mapping a prompt describing the bargaining scenario to their implemented strategy for negotiation.
Analysis of gradations of prompts shows that incremental emphasis of game features can reduce blatant mistakes.
To evaluate their relative performance, we formulate and estimate an empirical \textit{meta-game} model over the LLM agents.
We identify equilibria in this game model, and analyze the agents' competitiveness and fairness in the equilibrium context according to measures of regret, welfare, and envy-freeness.
Uncertainty in these estimates is quantified using a statistical bootstrapping approach.
Across nine LLM variants plus RL and heuristic baselines, OpenAI models (especially o3-mini) deliver the best individual and social performance, as well as greatest robustness. 
Introducing a heuristic rigid bargaining strategy exposes systematic vulnerabilities in some LLM agents, underscoring the value of adversarial evaluation.  
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) are typically evaluated with respect to static benchmarks, where performance can be measured in terms of quality of response to given inputs.
For domains with multiple interacting LLM agents, performance inherently depends on the behavior of other agents in the environment.
The evaluation approach, therefore, must carefully consider its assumptions about the other-agent context and examine the sensitivity of results to these strategic assumptions.

We undertake an evaluation of LLM agents for a particular strategic domain: sequential bargaining to allocate a set of items. 
Negotiation provides an appealing challenge problem for multiagent evaluation---a canonical scenario with plausible real-world application, where agents pursue self-interest but can succeed only by finding deals of mutual benefit.
It is thus a \textit{mixed-motive} domain, simultaneously involving competition over surplus and cooperative concession to reach agreement.

Before one would deploy an AI negotiation agent, one would wish to have confidence that the agent understands the principal's circumstance and objectives, and is effective in reaching beneficial deals.
From a societal perspective, we have additional acute interests in ensuring that:
\begin{itemize}
\item AI negotiators are effective in identifying creative deals for mutual benefit;
\item interactions between separately trained AI negotiators do not produce unanticipated negative outcomes;
and
    \item AI negotiators behave in a transparent and trustworthy manner (e.g., that they do not misrepresent facts or express insincere commitments).
\end{itemize}

Our investigation utilizes sequential bargaining not simply as a task of intrinsic interest, but as a controlled framework to examine emergent cooperation in contemporary LLMs. 
We adopt a classic two-agent multi-item bargaining setting employed in various AI studies (e.g., by \citet{Lewis17}).
Our version includes two key elements:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A \term{discount factor}, systematically reducing payoffs over negotiation rounds to incentivize rapid agreement.
\item An explicit \term{outside option}, allowing agents to opt out of negotiations and receive some value.
This requires agents to assess whether a mutually beneficial deal is feasible.
\end{enumerate}
Without discounting or outside options, short-horizon negotiation can degenerate into an ultimatum game.

\section{Background and Related Work}

Bargaining scenarios epitomize mixed-motive strategic interactions, requiring agents to consider their own interests and those of their counterpart. 
% Such scenarios support well-defined social welfare optima and permit rigorous evaluation using fairness metrics like Nash welfare and envy-freeness up to one item (EF1) \citep{Lipton2004Approximately}. 
% Consequently, we use bargaining as an analytical framework to assess the interplay of competition and cooperation in modern AI systems.
Our study investigates a multi-item bargaining scenario, related to a formulation popularized within AI by \citet{Lewis17} and employed subsequently in works by many researchers \citep{cao2018emergent,hua2024game,li2024meta,li25combining,lanctot2019openspiel,he-etal-2018-decoupling,chawla-etal-2023-selfish}. 

In the context of single-agent evaluation,  \citet{jordan2020evaluating} highlight the importance of robust benchmarks for evaluating agent capabilities.
These are likewise essential in multiagent settings, which also pose additional considerations particular to the strategic interaction.
Recent ``reevaluations'' \citep{balduzzi2018reevaluating, liu2025reevaluating} underscore the inadequacy of coarse metrics like win rates in capturing nuanced agent behaviors. 
Work in evaluating cooperation \citep{papoudakis2020benchmarking,sinha2019incentivizing}  emphasizes the need for welfare-centric evaluations.

Outcomes in multiagent environments inherently depend on the joint behavior of all agents. 
A particular agent's performance, therefore, hinges on which agents it plays against: the \term{strategic context}.
\citet{balduzzi2018reevaluating} argue that the most relevant strategic contexts are where other agents are approximately rational, and advocate evaluation with respect to a game-theoretic equilibrium, which they term \term{Nash-averaging}.
This is equivalent to the concept of \term{NE-regret}, employed by \citet{jordan2007empirical} to evaluate agents in a research competition.

\citet{li2024meta} introduce a meta-game evaluation approach, which uses empirical game-theoretic analysis (EGTA) \citep{Wellman25tg} to construct a game model over agent meta-strategies based on simulation data.
This work employs statistical bootstrapping techniques \citep{davison1997bootstrap} to provide distributional assessments of game-theoretic properties \citep{wiedenbeck2014bootstrap}.

The advent of GPT-class LLMs has prompted large-scale evaluations in competitive and cooperative games. 
Empirical probes reveal emergent strategic reasoning \citep{chen2023money,fan2024can,duan2024gtbench} and diverse behavioral patterns cataloged in \textsc{AgentBench} \citep{liu2024agentbench}.

\citet{hua2024game} study LLM bargaining in a setup similar to that considered here, focusing on the engineering of prompts to specify a workflow for decision making. 
They likewise evaluate outcomes based on welfare and fairness metrics, which in this case arise from the specified strategy as implemented through the LLM.
\citet{abdelnabi2024negotiation} propose a broader negotiation benchmark demanding a variety of agent skills. 
% Though performance-centric, these works initiate discussions on efficiency, fairness, and welfare—topics we systematically explore further.

Parallel social science research employs LLMs to simulate human-like participants, identifying biases and interaction heuristics \citep{horton2023large,argyle2023out,aher2023using}. 
These findings emphasize the need to audit LLM interactions beyond competitiveness, incorporating fairness and welfare considerations.

Emergent cooperation among multiagent LLM systems has also been explored, for instance by \citet{piatti2024cooperate} through societal simulations. 

% Nevertheless, systematic integration of theoretical fairness and welfare constructs into LLM evaluation remains limited relative to other evaluation domains. 
% Our study measures these concepts, leveraging cardinal utilities, observable outcomes, and controllable outside options, thus enabling robust evaluation of both agent performance and broader societal implications.

\section{Technical Preliminaries}
%\section{Bargaining Game and Game Theory Preliminaries}
\label{sec:game-framework} 


%We refer to the agents as player~1 and player~2, with player~1 taking the first action in each \term{round}.

\paragraph{Bargaining scenario.} 
The task on which we evaluate our agentified LLMs is an alternating-offer multi-item bargaining scenario between two agents, termed player~1 and player~2.
We have a fixed number $T$ of distinct item types $\itemtypes=\{1,\dots,T\}$ with $q_t$ items of type $t \in \itemtypes$. 
At the start of each bargaining instance, we independently sample, for each $t \in \itemtypes$, $q_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, defining a quantity vector $q = (q_1,\dots,q_T)$. %$\in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}^T$; 
For each player~$i\in\{1,2\}$, we independently draw $v_{i,t}\sim U(\{1,\dots,V\})$ for every $t\in\itemtypes$, defining a \term{private valuation} vector $v_i = (v_{i,1},\dots,v_{i,T})$, as well as an \term{outside-option value} $w_i \sim U(\{1,\dots,v_i\cdot q\})$.
% The parameters $\lambda, V$ are positive integers. 
%which the player obtains if negotiation fails. 
Each agent observes its own valuation and outside option, but not the other's. 
An \term{allocation} $a=(a_1,a_2)\in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}^{T}\times \mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}^{T}$ represents a partition of the set of items into \term{bundles} assigned to the agents: $a_1+a_2=q$. 

Negotiation unfolds over a maximum of $R$ \textit{rounds}, with player~1 taking the first action in each round. 
% In each round, agent 1 proposes first, followed by agent 2. 
On its turn, each player may choose to make an \OFFER\ in the form of an allocation, \ACCEPT\ the latest offer from the other, or \WALK\ away. 
The game ends upon an \ACCEPT\ or \WALK\ action, or after $R$ rounds; a final-round (counter)offer by player~2 is thus equivalent to \WALK. 
\ACCEPT\ results in an allocation $a$ corresponding to the accepted offer, valued by player~$i$ at $v_i \cdot a_i$.
Otherwise negotiation fails, and player $i$ obtains its outside-option value $w_i$. 
Values are geometrically discounted over rounds at a rate $\gamma \in (0,1)$, so that if the game ends in round $r$, each agent's payoff is the value of its outcome times the factor~$\gamma^{r-1}$.

% We will now introduce game-theoretic terminology adapted to our meta-game framework.

\paragraph{Strategies and utilities.} 
%On its turn, the agent selects from its set of available actions. 
%A \term{pure strategy} $\pi_i \in \Pi_i$ specifies a deterministic action for every possible state where agent~$i$ makes a decision. 
Let $-i$ denote the agent other than player~$i\in\{1,2\}$. A \term{pure strategy} \(\pi_i\in\Pi_i\) is a policy that assigns to player~\(i\) a possibly stochastic choice over its available actions at each decision point (i.e., its turn in a round).
A pure strategy in our framework is implemented by an LLM, an RL-derived policy, or a hand-crafted heuristic, some of which produce stochastic outputs (Section~\ref{sec:agent_strat}). 
A \term{mixed strategy} $\sigma_i \in \Delta(\Pi_i)$ is a probability distribution over pure strategies.
Player~$i$'s \term{utility} for playing $\pi_i$ against other-player strategy $\pi_{-i}$ is given by $u_i(\pi_i,\pi_{-i})$.
This utility is an expectation of realized payoffs with respect to the instance-specific item, valuation, and outside-option draws, as well as stochasticity of actions induced by the strategy.
For a mixed-strategy \term{profile} $\sigma = (\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$, player~$i$'s utility is defined as $u_i(\sigma) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi \sim \sigma}[u_i(\pi)]$.

\paragraph{Regret and equilibrium.} 
Player~$i$'s \term{regret} in profile~$\sigma$ is its maximum possible gain from unilateral deviation:
$\Regret_i(\sigma) = \max_{\pi_i' \in \Pi_i} u_i(\pi_i', \sigma_{-i}) - u_i(\sigma)$.
Strategy profile~$\sigma^*$ is a \term{Nash equilibrium} (NE) if no player can profitably deviate: $\Regret_1(\sigma^*) = \Regret_2(\sigma^*) =0$.

For games with multiple NE, we focus on the \term{maximum-entropy Nash equilibrium} (MENE), defined as the NE $\sigma^*$ that maximizes Shannon entropy $-\sigma^* \cdot \ln (\sigma^*)$. 
We approximate MENE using the mixed-integer linear programming approach of \citet{li2024meta} (with a tolerance of $10^{-6}$).

 
While regret helps us gauge outcomes from each agent's selfish-rational perspective, we use the following metrics to assess social desirability.

\paragraph{Welfare and fairness metrics.} 
For each outcome, we compute two social welfare measures: the \term{utilitarian welfare} (UW) and the \term{Nash welfare} (NW) which are the sum and the geometric mean of the agents' realized values respectively; maximizing the latter is known to balance efficiency with fairness \citep{Caragiannis2019Unreasonable}.
%We also consider a fairness metric, \emph{EF1 frequency}, the proportion of allocations that are envy-free up to one item, that is, $ v_i(a_j)-v_i(a_i)\le\max_{\theta\in a_j}v_i({\theta}) $ for every ordered pair of agents $(i,j)$ \citep{Lipton2004Approximately}.

We also assess fairness of negotiation outcomes using the envy-based paradigm.
An allocation is \term{envy-free} if each agent's valuation of its own allocated bundle is not lower than its valuation of every other agent's bundle. 
Since an envy-free allocation does not exist in general for indivisible items, we employ a relaxation which always exists: \term{envy-freeness up to one item} (EF1) \citep{Lipton2004Approximately,Budish11}. 
An allocation is EF1 if, for every agent $i$, there is an item in other-agent $-i$'s bundle which, if hypothetically removed, would make $i$ value its own bundle at least as much as $-i$'s diminished bundle. 
For our setting with additive valuations, this reduces to the condition $ v_i \cdot a_{-i} -v_i \cdot a_i\le\max_{k:a_{-i,k > 0}}v_{i,k}$ for $i\in\{1,2\}$. %Any envy that agent $1$ might feel toward $1$'s bundle can be eliminated by removing at most one item from $2$'s bundle. 
We then define \term{EF1 frequency} as the fraction of all  bargaining instances ending in \ACCEPT\ that produce EF1 allocations. %(instances that end in \WALK\ are excluded as there is no allocation to assess fairness). 

\paragraph{Empirical meta-game analysis.}
Our evaluation methodology follows the empirical meta-game analysis approach of \citet{li2024meta}.
We simulate all combinations of a set of negotiation agents in various game settings, and assemble the data in an empirical game model \citep{Wellman25tg}.
We simulate each agent in the roles of player~1 and player~2, and define a symmetric version of the game where the agents are randomized into these roles.
From the game model, we derive empirical equilibria, and employ these equilibria as strategic contexts for evaluation of the slate of agents.

So that our context is uniquely defined, we focus on MENE in particular, and measure each agent's characteristics in terms of its performance against the MENE profile. 
This includes regret metrics as well as welfare and fairness properties as described above.
To quantify uncertainty (e.g., standard errors), we take a bootstrapping approach, where the empirical game model is resampled and all measures are recalculated.
That is, for each game sample, we compute an associated MENE profile and calculate the performance measures with respect to that MENE opponent mixture.

\paragraph{Prompting circles (0–6).}
We organize our bargaining prompts into seven nested levels---called
“circles”---each adding a layer of strategic guidance.  
Appendix~\ref{app:mistakes} shows that certain moves in our game are
\term{mistakes} strictly worse than another available
action.  
We crafted our prompt hierarchy to provide high-level guidance and circumvent 
those mistakes, not to script optimal play; our objective is to
evaluate the models’ inherent  competence in mixed-motive settings, and not design workflows incorporating these models tailored to the specific task in this paper. See Appendix~\ref{app:prompting} for full descriptions of the circles.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Circle 0}: bare rules: items, private valuations, outside options, available actions.
  \item \textbf{Circle 1}: add the objective: maximize your own realized valuation at the negotiation outcome, considering your outside option. %payoff versus the outside option.
  \item \textbf{Circle 2}: show a worked numeric comparison before counteroffering.
  \item \textbf{Circle 3}: prescribe a step-by-step routine: assess, compare, then accept, walk, or propose.
  \item \textbf{Circle 4}: list five common mistakes (e.g., accepting worse-than-fallback offers).
  \item \textbf{Circle 5}: supply quick numeric checks that guard against those mistakes.
  \item \textbf{Circle 6}: encourage the agent to consider its opponent’s valuation from their offers and adjust.
\end{itemize}


We evaluated each LLM–circle pairing to be employed as a strategy in our meta-game framework on the five \term{mistakes} defined in Appendix~\ref{app:mistakes}, choosing for each model the  circles that suppressed mistakes.

We adopt the following naming convention for our LLM-circle pairings: \texttt{<LLM>-c-<k>}, where \texttt{<LLM>} is the model identifier (\texttt{sonnet-3.7}, for Anthropic Claude 3.7 “Sonnet”, \texttt{sonnet-3.7-r}, for Anthropic Claude 3.7 “Sonnet Reasoning”, \texttt{4o} for GPT-4o, \texttt{o3-mini} for OpenAI o3-mini, \texttt{gem-2.0-f} for Gemini 2.0 Flash); \texttt{c} stands for "circle"; and \texttt{<k>} indexes which circle was applied.
%Thus, for instance, \texttt{sonnet-3.7-c-5} is Claude Sonnet 3.7 with the prompt for circle 5. 

\subsection{Heuristic Strategies}
To create clear behavioral baselines and explore boundary conditions, we implemented two rule-based heuristic strategies:

\textbf{Tough Agent}: This heuristic implements an extreme strategy by consistently offering only one of its least-valued item in every counteroffer and never revising its position, testing other agents' responses to highly rigid unfavorable negotiation stances.

\textbf{Soft Agent}: Representing the opposite extreme, this agent unconditionally accepts any offer received. 
When forced to propose, it randomly generates offers without strategic intent.

These heuristic agents serve as benchmarks at opposite extremes of the negotiation spectrum.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning}

As a learning-based baseline, we implemented \term{Neural Fictitious Self-Play} (NFSP) \citep{heinrich2016deep}, which combines fictitious play in extensive-form games \citep{heinrich2015fictitious} with neural-network-based function approximation.
NFSP maintains two distinct policies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{best-response policy}, trained off-policy via Deep Q-Network (DQN) \citep{mnih2013playing} to exploit the current empirical average behavior of its opponents
  \item An \textbf{average policy}, derived through reservoir sampling \citep{vitter1985random} of the agent’s historical actions, which approximates equilibrium play.
\end{itemize}
In our experiments, we utilize the average policy.

Although NFSP is theoretically well suited for multiagent equilibrium learning, the complexity of our five-item bargaining game, with stochastic outside options, exhibits a state-action space substantially larger than the poker domains where NFSP was initially validated. 
Consequently, the NFSP policy employed in this study is not optimal due to computational limits.


\section{Experimental Setup}
In our experiments, we set our parameters as follows: $T=5$, $\lambda=4$, $V=100$, $\gamma \in \{0.90,0.98\}$, and $R \in \{3,5\}$. In total, we simulated 35,785 bargaining game instances across three settings of the bargaining game (BG):

We first evaluate head-to-head matchups among nine LLM variants across three distinct bargaining games, characterized by different discount factors ($\gamma$) and round limits ($R$). 
For each game, we generate 1,000 bootstrap resamples of trajectory data. 
In every bootstrap instance, we construct an empirical meta-game and compute its corresponding MENE.

Each agent's performance is assessed relative to this MENE across four key metrics:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Regret with respect to the MENE
\item Normalized Nash welfare
\item Normalized utilitarian welfare
\item Frequency of EF1 (envy-free up to one item) allocations.
\end{enumerate}

To compute the normalization constants for the welfare functions, we generated millions of game instances. 
For each game instance, we calculated its maximum possible Nash and utilitarian welfare over all possible outcomes (including falling back on outside options). 
We then used the average of the maxima of each welfare function over all instances as the corresponding normalization constant in our bootstrap analysis. 

This bootstrap approach generates distributions of equilibrium-adjusted outcomes rather than single-point estimates, enabling robust and context-rich agent comparisons within the broader strategic environment.

Aggregate results are presented in Table \ref{tab:bg_all_llm_only}, while Fig.~\ref{fig:llm_br_123} and Fig.~\ref{fig:bg0_br} illustrate the corresponding average best-response (BR) graphs.

\subsection{Empirical Results}
\label{sec:llm-empirical-results}

\textbf{OpenAI Models Performance.}
\texttt{o3-mini-c-0} consistently achieves the lowest MENE regret (2–5) across all games, while \texttt{4o} variants form the next-best group (regret 7–26). These models also achieve the highest normalized Nash welfare (90–93\%) and high EF1 frequencies, demonstrating strong alignment between equilibrium-seeking and welfare fairness.

\textbf{Regret-Welfare Relationship.}
Agents with regret below 10 consistently induce normalized Nash welfare above 89\% and rank in the top quartile for EF1 frequency across all bargaining games.

%\textbf{Gemini: Fair but Exploitable.}
%\texttt{gemini-2.0-f-c-2} achieves high EF1 frequency but elevated regret (above 29) and lower welfare scores. Average best-response graphs reveal vulnerability, with many strong counter-strategies targeting Gemini. 

\textbf{Anthropic Sonnet: Horizon-Dependent Performance.}
The reasoning-enhanced \texttt{sonnet-3.7-r-c-0} improves dramatically from regret 39.14 (BG1) to 12.36 (BG3), while the non-reasoning variant shows opposite trends. Longer horizons favor reasoning capabilities.

\subsection{Behavioral Analysis}
\label{sec:llm-only-games-qual-findings}

\textbf{Strategic Patterns.}
Few edges surpass the solidity threshold, indicating broad MENE mixing. However, \texttt{o3-mini-c-0} consistently attracts numerous incoming edges with minimal outgoing ones, highlighting its bargaining strength and low regret.

\textbf{Negotiation Strategies.}  
OpenAI's \texttt{o3-mini-c-0} adopts strategic opt-out behavior: walking in over 90\% of negotiations when the sum of outside offers exceeds the maximum achievable joint utility from an agreement, but only 10–37\% of the time when agreement would yield higher total welfare. Gemini opens with near-zero proposals, prolonging negotiations and achieving worse outcomes. Anthropic Sonnet balances walking and bargaining behaviors.

\textbf{Parameter Sensitivity.}
Game parameters critically shape interactions. BG1 ($\gamma=0.90$, $R=3$) yields only dashed relationships, while higher discount factors (BG2: $\gamma=0.98$) and extended rounds (BG3: $R=5$) produce solid best-response edges between specific model pairs.

\subsection{Key Takeaways}
OpenAI models, particularly \texttt{o3-mini-c-0} and GPT-4o variants, simultaneously achieve low regret, high welfare, and strong fairness. Gemini's tough bargaining remains vulnerable to exploitation, while Anthropic Sonnet's performance varies significantly with prompt modifications. The average best-response graphs confirm \texttt{o3-mini-c-0}'sis a top counter-strategy with persistent best-response relationships emerging between other vendor pairs.

\subsection{Empirical Results}
\label{sec:llm-nonllm-empirical-results}

\texttt{o3-mini-c-0} scores the lowest MENE regret
(1.63–4.28) and the highest Nash welfare (\(>92\%\) in BG0 and BG3), while the \texttt{4o} variants (\texttt{4o-c-5/6}) occupy the same regret–welfare frontier.

\textbf{Impact of Adversarial Baseline.}
Across all BGs, \texttt{tough} attracts the
largest number of incoming edges—often solid—
meaning it is a best response to many other agents.  Numerically, 6 distinct agents have a relatively high probability \(p>0.33\) with \texttt{tough} as a best response.
Yet \texttt{tough} itself suffers high regret
(\(>95\))  robust agents
(e.g., \texttt{o3-mini}, \texttt{4o-c-4/5/6}) are not exploited by it in turn, keeping its equilibrium payoff low. 

\textbf{OpenAI models are \emph{not} countered by \texttt{tough}.} No solid arrow points \texttt{from} an OpenAI strategy \texttt{into} \texttt{tough}; conversely, \texttt{o3-mini} and \texttt{4o} still receive a fair number of light incoming edges—albeit fewer and thinner than those pointing to \texttt{tough}.

\textbf{Gemini and Sonnet remain exploitable.} \texttt{Gem-2.0-f-c-2/5} and \texttt{sonnet-3.7-\{c-6,r-c-0\}}
still earn high fairness (EF1) scores but now
draw solid or dashed edges \emph{into}
\texttt{tough}, revealing that the adversarial baseline
is an effective counter-punch to them.
Their MENE regret therefore sits 3–4× higher
than that of the OpenAI cohort. 

\textbf{Baselines widen the performance spread.}
Adding \texttt{nfsp}, \texttt{soft}, and \texttt{tough} expands
the regret range from and pushes Nash welfare 
for the worst agents below \(70\%\) in some cases,
making differences among the LLMs more significant than in the LLM-only pool. 

\textbf{NFSP matters only in BG1.}
Interestingly, with the short horizon ($\gamma\!=\!0.9, R\!=\!3$) it grabs $\sim$11 \% of the equilibrium, on par with mid-tier OpenAI models.   
Its share greatly drops in BG2 and BG3.

\subsection{Key Takeaways}
Our results show that some models—especially \texttt{o3-mini-c-0} and the GPT-4o are robust to the \texttt{tough} strategy, while other LLM agents seem to be exploited by it and accept offers from it. Results also further show that OpenAI models still strong in terms of MENE regret and are overwhelmingly performant in terms of welfare and fairness metrics. 
\section{Conclusion}

We report results from an empirical study of LLM behavior in
a canonical strategic setting: multi-item bargaining games with discounting and outside
options.  
Pairing cardinal utilities with bootstrap meta-games lets us
analyze welfare and fairness side-by-side, making 
trade-offs explicit.  
Across 35,785 simulated negotiations,
OpenAI models (o3-mini, GPT-4o) achieve strong performance of
minimal MENE regret, top-quartile Nash and utilitarian welfare, and high EF1 fairness—and they account for nearly all support in the maximum-entropy Nash equilibrium—while remaining resilient to the adversarial \textsc{Tough} strategy.  
By contrast, Gemini and Claude are seemingly more exploitable.
